A . P . J Abdul Kalam

1931 - 2015

Missile Man of India

Dr. Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam, popularly known as A. P. J. Abdul Kalam, remains one of India’s most beloved and respected figures. A visionary scientist, dedicated educator, and former President of India, Dr. Kalam's life is an inspiring journey of perseverance, service, and humility. Born on 15 October 1931 in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, into a modest Tamil Muslim family, Kalam grew up in a challenging environment. Despite financial constraints, he developed a strong work ethic early in life, supporting his family by distributing newspapers. Driven by a passion for learning, he pursued physics and later aerospace engineering, ultimately graduating from the Madras Institute of Technology. Kalam began his career at the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) and later joined the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO). His significant contribution to India's space and missile programs earned him the nickname “Missile Man of India.” He played an instrumental role in the development of the Satellite Launch Vehicle (SLV), the Integrated Guided Missile Development Programme (IGMDP), and the Pokhran-II nuclear tests of 1998. His work not only strengthened India’s defense capabilities but also symbolized the country’s growing scientific prowess. In 2002, Dr. Kalam was elected as the 11th President of India, becoming the first scientist and the first bachelor to hold the nation’s highest constitutional post. His presidency was marked by a deep connection with the public, especially the youth, earning him the title “People’s President.” He advocated for education, innovation, and national development, often emphasizing his vision of India 2020—a blueprint for transforming India into a developed nation. Even after his presidency ended in 2007, Dr. Kalam remained actively involved in teaching and mentoring students across the country. He took up various academic roles, delivered lectures, and launched initiatives such as the “What Can I Give Movement” to inspire the younger generation to combat corruption and serve the nation selflessly. Dr. Kalam passed away on 27 July 2015 while delivering a lecture at IIM Shillong—symbolically, in the service of education and youth. His death was mourned across the nation, and he was laid to rest with full state honors in his hometown, Rameswaram. A memorial now stands there in his honor, showcasing his life and achievements. Known for his simplicity, humility, and deep spirituality, Dr. Kalam lived a modest life despite his towering achievements. A vegetarian, teetotaler, and lifelong bachelor, he dedicated his life to science, education, and the betterment of society. He left behind a legacy not just of scientific achievements, but of inspiration and national pride. In conclusion, Dr. A. P. J. Abdul Kalam's life story is a shining example of how dedication, knowledge, and integrity can transform an individual into a national icon. His journey from a small town in Tamil Nadu to the Rashtrapati Bhavan continues to motivate generations of Indians to dream big, work hard, and serve their country with honor.
